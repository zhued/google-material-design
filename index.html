Introduction

The modern world can’t seem to live without their phones and computers. So much can be done with this technology, and we rely so heavily on the results to show up right at our fingertips. But these “results” are only useful when we can understand and comprehend each action, reaction, and outcome on our screens.


Technology, phones, and gadgets have surrounded me for most of my life, and one recurring factor that I constantly see in many applications is inconsistent design. One portion of a website may have a certain format, yet another end may have a completely different layout. Apps may seem unresponsive because it fails to show any feedback to the users with what it’s doing.


Google, however, has a vision for clear, consistent, and responsive design guidelines. They call it Material Design.





“Material” guidelines revolve around creating a visual language that encompasses good design and unifies the same designs across different platforms and device sizes. Although most prominent in Google Apps on mobile and web, many designers have taking the initiative to incorporate material design into their apps, especially on Android market, to create a more central, unified environment.


Different principles of Material Design can be found in the side bar to the left. This site, although not perfect, incorporates this design within a browser to the best of its abilities.


Properties/Layouts

We see in a 3D world, where physical matter has distance from the user, some closer and some farther away; this creates a perception and references of space. However, our computer or phone screens are pretty flat, making it difficult to emulate this natural vision of humans.


Why don’t we make apps be 3D then? Have 3D objects of different thicknesses and sizes to make the screen POP!!




This is why we don’t. Just making objects pop out is unnatural for a flat screen, and quite frankly, it looks terrible.

Material design doesn’t use 3D objects, but fills in a 3D space where all objects have an x, y, and z dimension as well as a uniform thickness of 1 dp (pretty much one pixel of thickness).




This creates shadows from a “virtual” light, something that users are visibly familiar with.



To users, this is much more natural to the eyes and our depth perception. Instead of popping out at us, it seems as though there is an underlying structure inside the screen, as though the base layer is deeper inside, and other objects float at different heights.

If something is farther away, then it’s less prominent or important, when it’s floating higher/closer to you, it means it should be the sight of attention and interact-able.





In the layout without shadows, its just flat and unclear as to what layer is more important. But as the shadows become more prominent, you can see the differences in elevation and different functions of each object.

Material Design creates a simple, yet intuitive layout that a majority of users will be familiar with and understand from the get-go.
