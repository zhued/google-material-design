Transitions are the epitome of the user's understanding of what is happening with the content on the screen. Although quite essential for esthetics transitions will also define how users will follow an application. If a user fails to follow what is going on, that is an immediate failure and a lost cause within the application; it doesn't even matter how good the information actually is, the user is already lost. Material takes transitions back to the basic levels of visual flow and fluidity to create an easy to understand and enjoyable experience.

Visual Continuity

On a screen there are incoming elements, outgoing elements, and shared elements.

Incoming elements are new objects that are transitioning into the page to be introduced.

Outgoing elements are objects that are no long relevant to the current context that must be removed.

Shared elements are objects that will remain persistant from the beginning to the end of a transition, such as navigation bars or action controls.

There must be visual acknowledgement of the incoming elements, like where they came from, what they are supposed to do, and how they can be interacted. But once the back button is pressed, that material becomes outgoing elements, meaning they must be removed or put back into the place they orgininally once were; essentially reversing the order. In the example above, taping onto one of the music albumns will bring up the page with music and interatable buttons on playing the songs and more. But when the back button is pressed, everything returns to the original state of showing all the albumns.

It is imperative that these visual transitions stay continuous through the entire interaction to provide the users with a consistant model of the application to make it easier to navigate with more use.

Elevation

Material objects are NOT exactly 3D, but LIVE in a 3D space. Attempting to create realistic 3D objects will eventually look distorted and horrendous. Elevation of objects on the z-axis is key to this 3D space, not the shape and height of objects.

Take a look at the buttons below. Hover over them. Statically they are already elevated, but interacting with them, we see even more prominence. They gain more focus from the user.

button

Below are three different implementations of elevation. Without the contrasting pictures side by side, it is hard to tell the difference elevation makes on a visual. But it becomes very noticible within all three shots.

As users progress through each photo, it is visible which objects pop out more and have more importance and focus. The blue layer seems to float above the white space, probabaly making it a header of some sort, and the pink/purple button elevates above anything, as if there is an important action correlatd to it.

Shadows

Elevation creates shadows. Users have a mental model of distance through shadows in the real world, therefore recreating this characteristic helps the general user understand what is happening within the screen.
